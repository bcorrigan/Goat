Credit can usually be earned through John Carroll University, Cleveland State University, or Ashland University. Registration begins April 1, 2004. Please register early as our summer classes fill up quickly. Please call (216) 231-4600 ext 3211 or email the SRC with your questions. To register fill out the registration form and mail or fax with Museum fee.

Wonderful Wetlands
June 21-25, 2004, 9am-Noon
Instructor: Barb Kooser, Naturalist & Science-to-Go! instructor
Location: Carol H. Sweet Nature Center, Mentor Marsh Nature Preserve, Mentor, OH
Credit Hours: 1

Fees: Museum $150 plus University fee (ranges from $125 to $260 depending on the university)
Wetlands are wonderful places! Learn the functions and values of wetlands. Discover how plants and animals are adapted to living in wetlands. We will also discuss the natural history of Mentor Marsh in particular, and go out in the Marsh to identify plants and animals found there. No prior biology background is necessary for this class. Please dress appropriately for the weather. We will go into the Marsh so “wetable” footwear is advisable. Be aware that we may be in area with poison ivy, mud and biting/stinging insects. Long pants, hat and a change of shoes and socks are advised.

Volcanoes, Earthquakes, and Faulting for Teachers
July 5-9, 2004, 9:00am – Noon (exam on Monday July 12 , 9-10am)
Instructor: Dr. Joe Hannibal, Curator Invertebrate Paleontology
Location: Cleveland Museum of Natural History with field trip
Credit Hours: 1

Fees: Museum $150 plus University fee (ranges from $125 to $260 depending on the university)
This course will cover the basics of volcanoes, earthquakes and faulting. We will visit volcanoes and earthquakes by means of Cleveland Museum of Natural History exhibits, slides, and the internet. We will examine and learn to identify rock samples in the classroom and will learn to identify faults in the field near Cleveland. We will investigate historic earthquakes using old newspaper reports and see how earthquakes are recorded by OhioSeis, the Ohio Seismic network. We will also learn how to obtain information on volcanoes and earthquakes via the internet. Some simple activities useful in the classroom situations will be introduced.

Our Solar System
July 12-16, 2004, 9:00am – Noon
Instructor: Clyde Simpson, Observatory Coordinator
Location: Cleveland Museum of Natural History
Credit Hours: 1

Fees: Museum $150 plus University fee (ranges from $125 to $260 depending on the university)
You will learn the current scientific understanding of the origin, components and evolution of the solar system. We will use the new Nathan and Fannye Shafran Planetarium as a primary tool to explore the motions and exotic landscapes of the various bodies of the solar system. Weather permitting, the Museum's telescope in the Ralph Meuller Observatory will be employed to project daily images of the Sun. Other objects of the solar system will be investigated using exhibit displays throughout the Reinberger Hall of Earth and Planetary Exploration.

Local Flora
July 19-22,2004, 9:00am – 5:00 pm
Instructor: Robert Bartolotta, Outdoor Education Coordinator
Credit Hours: 2
Location: The Cleveland Museum of Natural History with daily field trips offsite
Fees: Museum $200 plus University fee (ranges from $250 to $520 depending on the university)

Learn plant identification outdoors in this Local Flora field course. The identification and recognition of trees, shrubs, ferns and herbaceous plants of northeastern Ohio will become second nature. Learn the characteristics of common families of plants. Practice using dichotomous, diagnostic keys. Experience first-hand the need for conservation and preservation of natural areas and native habitats. Learn to accurately describe the characteristics of leaves, stems, flowers, inflorescences, fruits and seeds. Successful completion of this two credit course will enable participants to teach others to recognize many species of plants, speak knowledgeably about the importance of plants in northeastern Ohio, write simple keys for plant identification, and converse in the language of botany. Please note: There are daily field trips. Transportation is provided by the Museum. Participants provide own lunch, snacks, and beverages daily. For the field trips please dress for the weather and be aware that we may be in area with poison ivy, mud and biting/stinging insects. Long pants, hat and a change of shoes and socks are advised.

Field Ecology for Teachers
August 9-1, 2004, 9:00am – 5:00pm
Instructor: Pam Keiper, SRC Coordinator
Credit Hours: 2
Location: The Cleveland Museum of Natural History with daily field trips
Fees: Museum $200 plus University fee (ranges from $250 to $520 depending on the university)

Ecology is the science the studies the relationships between living organisms and their enviroment. In this course we'll get outside to learn how the living and non-living elements of our world interact. Discover how plants and animals in the forest, meadow, wetland, and stream are adapted to living in Northeast Ohio. Populations and communities as well as competition, nutrient cycling/energy flow, and biodiversity will be discussed. Take simple, reproducible experiments back to your classroom. All can be adapted to K-12 classroom settings. Please note: There are daily field trips. Transportation is provided by the Museum. Participants provide own lunch, snacks, and beverages daily. For the field trips please dress for the weather and be aware that we may be in area with poison ivy, mud and biting/stinging insects. Long pants, hat and a change of shoes and socks are advised.

Lake Erie Ecology, Conservation, and Restoration Symposium
September 9-11,2004, 9:00am – 4:00pm
Facilitator: Pam Keiper, SRC Coordinator
Credit Hours: 1
Location: The Cleveland Museum of Natural History
Fees: Museum $15 plus University fee (approximately $145 Ashland University credit only)

Teachers rarely have the opportunity to participate in actual professional scientific meetings, the Lake Erie Ecology, Conservation, and Restoration symposium will allow teachers to interact with real Lake Erie ecologists and conservationists. Teachers will learn first-hand what the latest research shows about Ohio's largest lake by attending a field trip to one of the Museum's natural areas and by attending various presentations by Lake Erie scientists. Teachers will also be required to participate in a full day workshop where they will use scientific inquiry to explore the water cycle, aquatic organisms, watersheds, water pollution, and other water related topics. Teachers will be given the Project WET activity guide at the completion of the workshop. Grant funds may be available to defray fees and pay for substitute teachers.
